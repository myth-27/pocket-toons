=== Ghost Teller Episode 1 ===

  (Welcome, everyone. Take a seat. Make yourselves comfortable. Tonight, we're not here for your typical campfire tales.)
  (No sheet-wearing specters or rattling chains. No ancient curses or vengeful spirits haunting old houses.)
  (Tonight, we tell stories of a different kind of ghost. The ones that walk among us. The ones that wear human skin.)
  (The ones whose presence chills you to the bone, not because they’re dead, but because they’re so terrifyingly alive.)
  Storyteller 1: I have one. It happened to a friend of a friend, let's call her Minji. She was so excited, finally moving into her own small apartment in a quiet neighborhood.
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) Freedom! No more noisy roommates, no more shared kitchens. Just me and my own space.
  Storyteller 1: On her first day, as she was unpacking, there was a knock on her door.
  [KNOCK KNOCK]
  Mrs. Kim: Welcome, dear! I'm Mrs. Kim, your neighbor from across the hall. I brought some tteok for you. A housewarming gift.
  Minji: Oh, thank you so much, Mrs. Kim! That's so kind of you.
  Storyteller 1: Minji was touched. Mrs. Kim was an elderly woman, with a warm smile and kind eyes. She offered to help unpack, gave tips about the neighborhood.
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) This is great! I got lucky with such a sweet neighbor.
  Storyteller 1: But as days turned into weeks, Mrs. Kim's kindness started to feel... suffocating.
  Mrs. Kim: Did you sleep well, dear? You look a little tired today. Did you remember to eat breakfast?
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) How does she know if I look tired? She's always... there. Watching.
  [CLINK (of a spoon)]
  Storyteller 1: Minji would hear faint sounds from across the hall. A spoon clinking, a soft hum. Always when she was home.
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) Am I being paranoid? She's just an old lady.
  Storyteller 1: Then, one evening, Minji found a small, handwritten note slipped under her door.
  Minji: (Reading the note) 'Don't forget to lock your windows tonight, dear. It's supposed to rain heavily.'
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) Rain? I hadn't even checked the weather forecast. How did she know?
  [SHUDDER]
  Storyteller 1: A chill ran down Minji's spine. She started closing her blinds, feeling a desperate need for privacy.
  [CLICK (faint, from the door)]
  Minji: What was that? Did I hear something?
  Storyteller 1: She opened her door, but the hallway was empty. Just the faint smell of Mrs. Kim's cooking.
  Storyteller 1: The next day, she found a hand-knitted scarf on her doorknob. 'For the cold, dear,' a note said. But it wasn't cold.
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) This isn't kindness anymore. This is... something else.
  Storyteller 1: Minji started avoiding Mrs. Kim. She'd leave early, come home late, hoping to miss her. But Mrs. Kim was always there, a wave, a smile, a knowing look.
  Storyteller 1: Then, one afternoon, Minji came home. Her mail, which she'd left in the mailbox, was neatly stacked on her kitchen table.
  [GASP]
  Minji: What... how?
  Storyteller 1: And on her stove, a freshly brewed pot of herbal tea, still warm.
  Minji: (Trembling) Mrs. Kim! How did you get in?
  Mrs. Kim: (Smiling sweetly) Oh, dear. I was worried you weren't home. Your door was unlocked. I just wanted to make sure you were alright. And I tidied up a little.
  Minji (thinking): (Thought) My door was locked. I always lock it. Always.
  Storyteller 1: The smile on Mrs. Kim's face didn't falter. It was the smile of someone who genuinely believed they were doing good. But Minji saw it for what it was.
  Storyteller 1: The kindness was a cage. The care was surveillance. The neighbor, a ghost of suffocating presence.
  Storyteller 1: Minji moved out a week later. She never told Mrs. Kim. She just left.
  Storyteller 1: But even in her new apartment, she sometimes feels a lingering chill, a sense of being watched. And she double-checks her locks, every single night.
  Listener 1: That's... terrifying.
  Listener 2: I'd rather have a real ghost.
  (See? No ghosts. Just... people. And sometimes, that's far more unsettling.)
